1. Scope

A literal list of every chemical reaction in the human body is not a normal book-length object. Human genome-scale metabolic reconstructions contain more than ten thousand reactions, and pathway databases include not only small-molecule metabolism but also transport, binding, protein processing, signaling, transcription, translation, and repair. This book therefore gives a curated, readable map of the core human biochemical reactions, with references to databases that hold exhaustive machine-readable reaction sets.

Biochemistry is the chemistry of organized flux. Human cells do not simply contain chemicals; they maintain directed flows of carbon, nitrogen, electrons, phosphate groups, methyl groups, acyl groups, ions, and information. The same molecule can be fuel in one tissue, building material in another, and a signal somewhere else.

The most useful way to learn this chemistry is by pattern. A reaction such as phosphorylation appears in glycolysis, protein signaling, glycogen regulation, and nucleotide metabolism. Oxidation-reduction appears in energy metabolism, detoxification, collagen maturation, and antioxidant defense. Hydrolysis appears in digestion, ATP use, proteolysis, lipolysis, and nucleic-acid turnover.

2. The Chemical Grammar of Life

Human biochemical reactions are mostly ordinary organic and inorganic chemistry under extraordinary control. Enzymes lower activation energy, cofactors move electrons or groups, membranes separate incompatible chemistries, and regulation makes the pathway responsive to energy state, hormones, nutrients, oxygen, and stress.

Core Molecules

Water

H2O ↔ H+ + OH−

Water participates directly in hydrolysis, hydration, dehydration, proton transfer, and acid-base buffering.

Carbon dioxide buffer

CO2 + H2O ↔ H2CO3 ↔ HCO3− + H+

Carbonic anhydrase makes this fast enough to connect cellular respiration with blood pH and ventilation.

ATP

ATP + H2O → ADP + Pi + H+

ATP hydrolysis is coupled to transport, movement, biosynthesis, and regulation.

Redox currency

NAD+ + 2 e− + H+ ↔ NADH

NADH usually carries electrons to ATP production. NADPH usually powers reductive biosynthesis and antioxidant repair.

3. Carbohydrate Metabolism

Carbohydrates enter human metabolism as glucose, fructose, galactose, glycogen, glycoprotein sugars, and glycolytic intermediates. Glucose is central because it can feed ATP production, lactate generation, glycogen storage, pentose phosphate chemistry, lipid synthesis, amino acid synthesis, and glycosylation.

Digestion and Entry

Reaction Equation Description
Starch hydrolysis (C6H10O5)n + n H2O → n C6H12O6 Amylases and brush-border enzymes release absorbable monosaccharides.
Sucrose hydrolysis Sucrose + H2O → glucose + fructose Sucrase splits a disaccharide into two hexoses.
Lactose hydrolysis Lactose + H2O → glucose + galactose Lactase activity determines lactose tolerance.
Glucose phosphorylation Glucose + ATP → glucose-6-phosphate + ADP + H+ Hexokinase and glucokinase trap glucose inside cells and commit it to metabolism.

Glycolysis

Glycolysis converts glucose into pyruvate. In oxygen-rich cells, pyruvate usually enters mitochondria. In red blood cells, exercising muscle, and hypoxic tissues, pyruvate is reduced to lactate to regenerate NAD+.

Net: glucose + 2 ADP + 2 Pi + 2 NAD+ → 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP + 2 NADH + 2 H+ + 2 H2O

Step Equation Purpose
Hexokinase/glucokinase Glucose + ATP → glucose-6-phosphate + ADP + H+ Glucose capture; glucokinase supports hepatic glucose buffering.
Phosphoglucose isomerase Glucose-6-phosphate ↔ fructose-6-phosphate Aldose-ketose rearrangement prepares for symmetric cleavage.
Phosphofructokinase-1 Fructose-6-phosphate + ATP → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate + ADP + H+ Main committed glycolytic control step; stimulated by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate.
Aldolase Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate ↔ glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate + dihydroxyacetone phosphate Splits a six-carbon sugar into two three-carbon phosphates.
Triose phosphate isomerase Dihydroxyacetone phosphate ↔ glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate Converts both products into the oxidation-ready form.
GAP dehydrogenase Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate + Pi + NAD+ ↔ 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate + NADH + H+ Captures oxidation energy as a high-energy acyl phosphate.
Phosphoglycerate kinase 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate + ADP ↔ 3-phosphoglycerate + ATP Substrate-level phosphorylation.
Phosphoglycerate mutase 3-phosphoglycerate ↔ 2-phosphoglycerate Moves phosphate to prepare for dehydration.
Enolase 2-phosphoglycerate ↔ phosphoenolpyruvate + H2O Forms a high-energy enol phosphate.
Pyruvate kinase Phosphoenolpyruvate + ADP + H+ → pyruvate + ATP Second ATP-forming step; strongly regulated in liver.

Lactate, Cori Cycling, and Anaerobic Flux

Pyruvate + NADH + H+ ↔ lactate + NAD+

Lactate dehydrogenase protects glycolytic flux when mitochondrial oxidation is limited. Lactate is not merely waste; it is a circulating carbon carrier. Liver and kidney can convert it back to glucose, while heart and oxidative muscle can burn it.

Gluconeogenesis

Gluconeogenesis is not simple reverse glycolysis. Three irreversible glycolytic steps are bypassed with separate enzymes.

Bypass Equation Role
Pyruvate carboxylase Pyruvate + HCO3− + ATP → oxaloacetate + ADP + Pi Mitochondrial biotin enzyme; activated by acetyl-CoA.
PEP carboxykinase Oxaloacetate + GTP → phosphoenolpyruvate + GDP + CO2 Uses decarboxylation to help drive PEP formation.
Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate + H2O → fructose-6-phosphate + Pi Major regulated gluconeogenic step; inhibited by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate.
Glucose-6-phosphatase Glucose-6-phosphate + H2O → glucose + Pi ER enzyme in liver, kidney, and intestine; releases free glucose.

Approximate net: 2 pyruvate + 4 ATP + 2 GTP + 2 NADH + 6 H2O → glucose + 4 ADP + 2 GDP + 6 Pi + 2 NAD+ + 2 H+

Pentose Phosphate Pathway

The oxidative branch makes NADPH. The nonoxidative branch rearranges carbon skeletons so ribose-5-phosphate and glycolytic intermediates can be interconverted.

Transketolase/transaldolase 3 ribulose-5-phosphate ↔ 2 fructose-6-phosphate + glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate Carbon shuffling; transketolase requires thiamine pyrophosphate.

4. Mitochondria, Oxygen, and ATP

Mitochondria oxidize carbon fuels to CO2, reduce oxygen to water, and conserve a portion of the released free energy as a proton gradient and ATP. They also host key steps in gluconeogenesis, urea synthesis, heme synthesis, fatty acid oxidation, steroid metabolism, apoptosis, and innate immune signaling.

Pyruvate Dehydrogenase Complex

Pyruvate + CoA-SH + NAD+ → acetyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH + H+

Pyruvate dehydrogenase is a gate between carbohydrate oxidation and acetyl-CoA metabolism. It uses thiamine pyrophosphate, lipoamide, FAD, NAD+, and CoA. It is inhibited by phosphorylation, NADH, and acetyl-CoA, and activated by dephosphorylation when energy demand rises.

Citric Acid Cycle

Net: acetyl-CoA + 3 NAD+ + FAD + GDP + Pi + 2 H2O → 2 CO2 + CoA-SH + 3 NADH + FADH2 + GTP + 3 H+

Step Equation Meaning
Citrate synthase Oxaloacetate + acetyl-CoA + H2O → citrate + CoA-SH + H+ Condenses two-carbon acetyl with four-carbon oxaloacetate.
Aconitase Citrate ↔ cis-aconitate + H2O ↔ isocitrate Moves hydroxyl group to enable oxidation.
Isocitrate dehydrogenase Isocitrate + NAD+ → α-ketoglutarate + CO2 + NADH + H+ First oxidative decarboxylation.
α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase α-ketoglutarate + CoA-SH + NAD+ → succinyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH + H+ Second oxidative decarboxylation; mechanistically resembles PDH.
Succinyl-CoA synthetase Succinyl-CoA + GDP + Pi ↔ succinate + GTP + CoA-SH Substrate-level phosphorylation.
Succinate dehydrogenase Succinate + FAD → fumarate + FADH2 Only TCA enzyme embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane; Complex II.
Fumarase Fumarate + H2O ↔ malate Hydration of a double bond.
Malate dehydrogenase Malate + NAD+ ↔ oxaloacetate + NADH + H+ Regenerates oxaloacetate; pulled forward by citrate synthase.

Anaplerosis and Cataplerosis

The TCA cycle is not only a furnace. It is also a traffic circle. Intermediates leave for amino acids, glucose, heme, and lipids, and they must be replenished.

Route Equation Use
Pyruvate carboxylase Pyruvate + HCO3− + ATP → oxaloacetate + ADP + Pi Replenishes oxaloacetate for TCA flux or gluconeogenesis.
Glutamate dehydrogenase Glutamate + NAD(P)+ + H2O ↔ α-ketoglutarate + NH4+ + NAD(P)H + H+ Links amino nitrogen with the TCA cycle.
Propionyl-CoA entry Propionyl-CoA → D-methylmalonyl-CoA → L-methylmalonyl-CoA → succinyl-CoA Odd-chain fatty acids and several amino acids enter as succinyl-CoA.
Citrate export Citratematrix → citratecytosol → acetyl-CoA + oxaloacetate Provides cytosolic acetyl-CoA for fatty acid and cholesterol synthesis.

Electron Transport and Oxidative Phosphorylation

Complex Equation Description
I: NADH dehydrogenase NADH + H+ + Q → NAD+ + QH2 Transfers electrons from NADH to ubiquinone and pumps protons.
II: Succinate dehydrogenase Succinate + Q → fumarate + QH2 Feeds electrons from FADH2 into ubiquinone without proton pumping.
III: Cytochrome bc1 QH2 + 2 cytochrome c(Fe3+) → Q + 2 cytochrome c(Fe2+) + 2 H+ Q cycle moves electrons to cytochrome c and contributes to the proton gradient.
IV: Cytochrome c oxidase 4 cytochrome c(Fe2+) + O2 + 8 H+matrix → 4 cytochrome c(Fe3+) + 2 H2O + 4 H+IMS Reduces oxygen to water and pumps protons.
V: ATP synthase ADP + Pi + proton motive force → ATP + H2O Converts electrochemical gradient energy into phosphoryl-transfer potential.
Cyanide, carbon monoxide, hydrogen sulfide, and severe hypoxia impair oxygen-linked electron transport. The immediate biochemical problem is not lack of glucose; it is failure to reoxidize reduced cofactors and maintain ATP production.

5. Lipid Metabolism

Lipids store energy, build membranes, insulate nerves, transport fat-soluble vitamins, and generate hormones and inflammatory mediators. Their reactions are compartmentalized: cytosol for fatty acid synthesis, mitochondria for beta-oxidation, ER for many lipid modifications, peroxisomes for very-long-chain fatty acids, and mitochondria plus smooth ER for steroid synthesis.

Digestion, Transport, and Mobilization

Reaction Equation Description
Triacylglycerol hydrolysis Triacylglycerol + 3 H2O → glycerol + 3 fatty acids Lipases digest dietary fat and mobilize adipose stores.
Fatty acid activation Fatty acid + CoA-SH + ATP → acyl-CoA + AMP + PPi Costs two high-energy phosphate equivalents; commits fatty acid to metabolism.
Carnitine acyltransferase Acyl-CoA + carnitine ↔ acyl-carnitine + CoA-SH Transfers long-chain fatty acids into mitochondria.
Glycerol kinase Glycerol + ATP → glycerol-3-phosphate + ADP + H+ Important in liver; adipose lacks high glycerol kinase activity.

Beta-Oxidation

Each mitochondrial beta-oxidation cycle shortens a fatty acyl-CoA by two carbons and produces acetyl-CoA, FADH2, and NADH.

Step Equation Purpose
Acyl-CoA dehydrogenase Acyl-CoA + FAD → trans-Δ2-enoyl-CoA + FADH2 Introduces a double bond.
Enoyl-CoA hydratase trans-Δ2-enoyl-CoA + H2O → L-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA Hydrates the double bond.
Hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase L-3-hydroxyacyl-CoA + NAD+ → 3-ketoacyl-CoA + NADH + H+ Oxidizes an alcohol to a ketone.
Thiolase 3-ketoacyl-CoA + CoA-SH → acetyl-CoA + acyl-CoAn-2 Cleaves the beta-keto thioester.

Palmitoyl-CoA + 7 FAD + 7 NAD+ + 7 CoA-SH + 7 H2O → 8 acetyl-CoA + 7 FADH2 + 7 NADH + 7 H+

Fatty Acid Synthesis

Fatty acid synthesis is reductive, cytosolic, and fed by citrate export from mitochondria. NADPH comes mainly from the pentose phosphate pathway and malic enzyme.

Reaction Equation Description
ATP citrate lyase Citrate + CoA-SH + ATP → acetyl-CoA + oxaloacetate + ADP + Pi Generates cytosolic acetyl-CoA.
Acetyl-CoA carboxylase Acetyl-CoA + HCO3− + ATP → malonyl-CoA + ADP + Pi Committed step; malonyl-CoA inhibits carnitine palmitoyltransferase I.
Fatty acid synthase Acetyl-CoA + 7 malonyl-CoA + 14 NADPH + 14 H+ → palmitate + 7 CO2 + 8 CoA-SH + 14 NADP+ + 6 H2O Builds palmitate by repeated condensation, reduction, dehydration, and reduction.
Desaturase Acyl-CoA + NADH + H+ + O2 → unsaturated acyl-CoA + NAD+ + 2 H2O Introduces double bonds; humans cannot introduce omega-3 or omega-6 double bonds de novo.

Ketone Bodies

Ketogenesis occurs mainly in hepatic mitochondria during fasting, prolonged exercise, low insulin states, or carbohydrate restriction. Ketone bodies export acetyl units to extrahepatic tissues.

Reaction Equation Description
Thiolase 2 acetyl-CoA ↔ acetoacetyl-CoA + CoA-SH Condenses two acetyl units.
HMG-CoA synthase Acetoacetyl-CoA + acetyl-CoA + H2O → HMG-CoA + CoA-SH Rate-controlling hepatic ketogenesis enzyme.
HMG-CoA lyase HMG-CoA → acetoacetate + acetyl-CoA Produces the first ketone body.
Beta-hydroxybutyrate dehydrogenase Acetoacetate + NADH + H+ ↔ β-hydroxybutyrate + NAD+ Reflects mitochondrial redox state.
Spontaneous decarboxylation Acetoacetate → acetone + CO2 Produces volatile acetone.
Ketolysis Acetoacetate + succinyl-CoA → acetoacetyl-CoA + succinate Extrahepatic tissues activate ketones; liver lacks thiophorase for its own ketone use.

6. Amino Acids and Nitrogen

Nitrogen metabolism balances protein turnover, amino acid synthesis, neurotransmitter production, heme synthesis, nucleotide synthesis, methylation, and ammonia disposal. Free ammonia is toxic, so human metabolism usually moves nitrogen as glutamate, glutamine, alanine, aspartate, or urea.

Transamination and Ammonia Handling

Reaction Equation Description
General transamination Amino acid + α-ketoglutarate ↔ α-keto acid + glutamate PLP-dependent transfer of amino groups.
ALT Alanine + α-ketoglutarate ↔ pyruvate + glutamate Glucose-alanine cycle between muscle and liver.
AST Aspartate + α-ketoglutarate ↔ oxaloacetate + glutamate Feeds nitrogen to the urea cycle and links to malate-aspartate shuttle.
Glutamate dehydrogenase Glutamate + NAD(P)+ + H2O ↔ α-ketoglutarate + NH4+ + NAD(P)H + H+ Releases or captures ammonia in mitochondrial metabolism.
Glutamine synthetase Glutamate + NH4+ + ATP → glutamine + ADP + Pi Detoxifies ammonia and transports nitrogen safely.
Glutaminase Glutamine + H2O → glutamate + NH4+ Important in kidney acid-base balance and rapidly dividing cells.

Urea Cycle

The urea cycle converts ammonia and aspartate nitrogen into urea, mainly in liver. It spans mitochondria and cytosol.

Step Equation Location and note
Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase I NH4+ + HCO3− + 2 ATP → carbamoyl phosphate + 2 ADP + Pi Mitochondrial; requires N-acetylglutamate.
Ornithine transcarbamylase Ornithine + carbamoyl phosphate → citrulline + Pi Mitochondrial; citrulline exits to cytosol.
Argininosuccinate synthetase Citrulline + aspartate + ATP → argininosuccinate + AMP + PPi Cytosolic; adds the second urea nitrogen.
Argininosuccinate lyase Argininosuccinate → arginine + fumarate Fumarate links the urea cycle to the TCA cycle.
Arginase Arginine + H2O → ornithine + urea Completes the cycle; ornithine returns to mitochondria.

7. Nucleotides, DNA, and RNA Chemistry

Nucleotides are genetic letters, energy carriers, signaling molecules, activated sugars, lipid head-group donors, and coenzyme components. Their synthesis consumes amino acid nitrogen, one-carbon units, ribose phosphate, bicarbonate, and ATP.

9. Detoxification, Redox Defense, and Signaling

Cells constantly transform oxygen, xenobiotics, drugs, hormones, heme, bile pigments, and reactive intermediates. Detoxification is not a single pathway. It is a layered system of functionalization, conjugation, transport, and antioxidant repair.

Alcohol and Acetate

Reaction Equation Description

Acetyl-CoA synthetase Acetate + CoA-SH + ATP → acetyl-CoA + AMP + PPi Converts acetate into central metabolism.

10. Tissue-Level Integration

The same pathways are expressed differently across tissues. Human biochemistry is therefore both molecular and anatomical. A liver cell, red blood cell, neuron, adipocyte, kidney tubule cell, and skeletal muscle fiber make different choices from the same chemical menu.

Major Tissue Programs

Tissue Signature reactions Metabolic role
Liver Glucose-6-phosphate + H2O → glucose + Pi; NH4+ + HCO3− + aspartate → urea Blood glucose buffering, urea production, detoxification, bile acid synthesis, ketogenesis.
Skeletal muscle Phosphocreatine + ADP + H+ ↔ creatine + ATP; glycogen + Pi → glucose-1-phosphate Rapid ATP demand, glycogen storage for local use, amino acid export as alanine/glutamine.
Heart Fatty acyl-CoA + O2 → CO2 + H2O + ATP High continuous oxidative metabolism; flexible use of fatty acids, lactate, glucose, and ketones.
Brain Glucose → CO2 + H2O; β-hydroxybutyrate → acetyl-CoA Normally glucose-rich metabolism; uses ketones during fasting adaptation.
Adipose Triacylglycerol + 3 H2O → glycerol + 3 fatty acids; glycerol-3-phosphate + acyl-CoA → triacylglycerol Energy storage, endocrine signaling, fatty acid release.
Red blood cell Glucose → lactate; glucose-6-phosphate + NADP+ → NADPH No mitochondria; relies on glycolysis, pentose phosphate pathway, and glutathione.
Kidney Glutamine + H2O → glutamate + NH4+; lactate → glucose Acid-base balance, ammonium excretion, gluconeogenesis.

Fed, Fasting, and Exercise States

Fed: insulin favors glucose uptake, glycolysis, glycogen synthesis, fatty acid synthesis, protein synthesis, and storage.
Early fasting: glucagon and epinephrine favor hepatic glycogenolysis, adipose lipolysis, gluconeogenesis, and fatty acid oxidation.
Prolonged fasting: liver makes ketone bodies; kidney contributes more gluconeogenesis; muscle reduces glucose use to spare it for brain and red blood cells.
Exercise: ATP demand increases phosphocreatine use, glycogenolysis, glycolysis, TCA flux, fatty acid oxidation, lactate exchange, and amino acid turnover depending on intensity and duration.
