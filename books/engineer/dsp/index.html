DSP Basics: Signals, Convolution, Filters, and Audio Techniques

DSP studies signals as sequences of numbers and systems as transformations of those sequences. For audio, a signal is pressure variation over time sampled into a stream such as 48,000 numbers per second.

1. Signals

Concept Meaning Audio example Common mistake
Amplitude Instantaneous signal value. Normalized samples in [-1, 1]. Clipping when values exceed the output range.
Frequency Cycles per second, measured in hertz. 440 Hz is the A above middle C. Confusing angular frequency rad/sample with Hz.
Phase Position within a cycle. Phase shifts alter waveform alignment and transients. Ignoring phase when summing correlated signals.
Energy and power Magnitude accumulated over time or averaged over time. RMS loudness proxy. Using peak level as the only loudness statistic.
Noise Random or unwanted signal component. Hiss, hum, quantization noise, sensor noise. Filtering noise without checking whether signal shares the same band.
DSP contract: a digital signal is usually x[n], a sequence indexed by integer sample number. Time in seconds is t = n / sample_rate. Frequency in Hz maps to digital radian frequency as omega = 2*pi*f/sample_rate.

Signals, systems, and linear time invariance

A system maps an input signal to an output signal. A linear system respects scaling and addition. A time-invariant system behaves the same way regardless of when the input occurs. Linear time-invariant systems are special because they are completely described by their impulse response and can be analyzed by convolution and frequency response.

Three useful signal views

A waveform shows what happens over time, but it does not directly tell you which frequencies are present. A spectrum shows frequency content, but it hides when events happen. A spectrogram compromises by showing short-time spectra across time. Audio DSP usually moves between all three views: waveform for transients and clipping, spectrum for tone and filtering, and spectrogram for changing sounds such as speech, drums, chirps, and musical notes.

x(t) = A cos(2π f t + φ) x[n] = A cos(2π f n / fs + φ) The first line is a continuous-time sinusoid. The second is the sampled version at sample rate fs.

Complex sinusoids are the mathematical atoms of DSP. They are useful because linear time-invariant systems do not change their frequency, only their amplitude and phase. This is why the frequency response of a filter is so powerful: it says how every sinusoidal component is scaled and shifted.

2. Sampling and Aliasing

Sampling converts a continuous-time signal into a sequence. If the sampling rate is too low, high frequencies fold into lower frequencies as aliases.

Nyquist rule

To represent a sinusoid of frequency f, the sample rate must be greater than 2f. Real audio systems use anti-aliasing filters before sampling because real signals contain many frequencies.

Nyquist frequency = sample_rate / 2 alias(f, fs) = abs(((f + fs/2) mod fs) - fs/2)

Important consequences

  • 44.1 kHz audio can represent frequencies up to about 22.05 kHz.
  • Downsampling requires lowpass filtering first.
  • Aliasing is not just loss of quality. It creates new wrong frequencies.
  • Oversampling pushes artifacts higher, where they can be filtered more easily.

3. 1D Convolution

Convolution combines a signal with an impulse response. In audio, convolution can implement tone controls, echoes, room reverbs, moving averages, and many other linear effects.

Continuous time:   y(t) = (x * h)(t) = ∫-∞∞ x(τ) h(t - τ) dτ Discrete time:      y[n] = (x * h)[n] = ∑k=-∞∞ x[k] h[n - k] Both formulas say the same thing: the output is built by sliding one signal across another, multiplying overlap, and accumulating the result.

What convolution means

Imagine sending a very short impulse into a system and recording the output. That output is the impulse response. Any signal can be decomposed into shifted, scaled impulses. Convolution reconstructs the system output by adding shifted copies of the impulse response, each scaled by the input value at that shift.

In continuous time, the integral adds infinitely many shifted copies. In discrete time, the summation adds sample-spaced copies. The concept is identical, but digital audio works with finite buffers, so real implementations use finite sums, block processing, or FFT convolution for long impulse responses.

Why convolution is central

Convolution is the bridge between time-domain behavior and frequency-domain behavior. A filter's impulse response explains what the filter does to impulses, transients, echoes, and ringing. The Fourier transform of that impulse response explains what the filter does to sinusoidal components.

If   y(t) = x(t) * h(t),   then   Y(f) = X(f) H(f) If   y[n] = x[n] * h[n],   then   Y(ejω) = X(ejω) H(ejω) Convolution in time becomes multiplication in frequency. This is the reason filtering can be understood as spectral shaping.
Impulse response Effect Audio meaning
[1] Identity Output equals input.
[1, 0.6, 0.3] Echo taps Delayed repeats.
Moving average Lowpass smoothing Reduces high-frequency detail.
Windowed sinc FIR frequency-selective filter Designed lowpass, highpass, or bandpass.

Properties worth memorizing

Property Math-style statement DSP consequence
Commutative x * h = h * x The sliding interpretation can flip either signal; the result is the same.
Associative (x * h1) * h2 = x * (h1 * h2) Cascaded LTI filters have an equivalent single impulse response.
Distributive x * (h1 + h2) = x * h1 + x * h2 Parallel filters can be analyzed as summed impulse responses.
Identity x * delta = x The impulse is the neutral element of convolution.
Delay x[n] * delta[n - D] = x[n - D] A shifted impulse response delays the signal.
Implementation warning: the mathematical formulas assume infinite signals. Real code must choose boundary handling: zero padding, circular convolution, overlap-add, overlap-save, or stateful streaming. Many audio clicks and FFT artifacts come from using the wrong boundary model.

4. Analog and Digital Filters

Filters reshape spectra. Analog filters are described in continuous time, often with the Laplace variable s. Digital filters are described in discrete time, often with z transforms or difference equations.

Analog intuition

Analog filters are built from components such as resistors, capacitors, inductors, and op amps. A simple RC lowpass has a smooth rolloff and a cutoff controlled by the time constant.

RC lowpass: H(s) = 1 / (1 + sRC) cutoff fc = 1 / (2*pi*RC)

Digital implementation

Digital filters run on samples. FIR filters use only input samples. IIR filters use input samples and previous output samples, which gives feedback and can create efficient resonant responses.

FIR: y[n] = b0*x[n] + b1*x[n-1] + ... + bM*x[n-M] IIR: y[n] = b0*x[n] + ... + bM*x[n-M] - a1*y[n-1] - ... - aN*y[n-N]

FIR versus IIR

Filter type Strength Risk Typical uses
FIR Always stable, can be linear phase. May need many taps for sharp transitions. Equalization, resampling, linear-phase audio filters.
IIR Efficient sharp responses and resonances. Can become unstable if poles leave the unit circle. Audio EQ biquads, analog-style filters, control systems.
Analog prototype Continuous-time physical model. Needs discretization for digital processing. Butterworth, Chebyshev, Bessel designs before bilinear transform.

Magnitude, phase, and delay

A filter is not only a magnitude curve. It also has a phase response. Magnitude tells which frequencies get louder or quieter. Phase tells how sinusoidal components are shifted in time. Group delay describes how much delay a narrow frequency band experiences. This matters for transients, stereo imaging, crossover design, and any situation where multiple filtered paths are mixed back together.

H(ejω) = |H(ejω)| ejφ(ω) Group delay:   τg(ω) = - dφ(ω) / dω Linear-phase FIR filters keep group delay nearly constant. IIR filters are efficient, but their phase is usually nonlinear.

5. The Z-plane

The z-plane gives a geometric view of digital filters. Zeros reduce response near their angles. Poles increase response near their angles. Stability requires all poles to be inside the unit circle.

H(z) = (b0 + b1*z^-1 + ... + bM*z^-M) / (1 + a1*z^-1 + ... + aN*z^-N)

How to read it

  • The unit circle corresponds to frequency response.
  • Angle maps to digital frequency.
  • Radius of a pole controls decay time and resonance strength.
  • A pole near the unit circle creates a narrow peak.
  • A zero on the unit circle creates a deep notch.

Stability and causality

A causal IIR filter is stable when every pole is strictly inside the unit circle. Poles close to the unit circle can ring for a long time. Poles outside the unit circle grow, which can blow up audio or numeric state.

Poles and zeros as sound design controls

A zero near the unit circle removes energy at its angle, creating a notch or cancellation. A pole near the unit circle reinforces energy at its angle, creating resonance. Moving a pole outward narrows the bandwidth and lengthens ringing. Moving it inward shortens the decay and broadens the peak. Many familiar audio EQ sections are controlled pole-zero patterns packaged behind frequency, gain, and Q knobs.

6. DFT, FFT, Windows, and Spectrograms

The discrete Fourier transform expresses a finite signal as a weighted sum of complex sinusoids. The FFT is a fast algorithm for computing the same transform.

Fourier idea in one sentence

Fourier analysis asks how much of each sinusoidal frequency is present in a signal. A single sinusoid is simple in frequency and repetitive in time. A short click is simple in time and broad in frequency. Most real audio sits between those extremes.

Continuous Fourier transform:   X(f) = ∫-∞∞ x(t) e-j2πft dt Inverse transform:            x(t) = ∫-∞∞ X(f) ej2πft df The transform measures correlation with complex sinusoids. The inverse rebuilds the signal by adding those sinusoids back together.

For discrete-time signals, the frequency variable is periodic. Frequencies separated by one full turn around the unit circle are equivalent. This is why digital frequency is often written as ω radians per sample and why the z-plane unit circle matters.

DTFT:   X(ejω) = ∑n=-∞∞ x[n] e-jωn Inverse:   x[n] = (1 / 2π) ∫-ππ X(ejω) ejωn dω The DTFT is continuous in frequency but periodic every 2π radians/sample.

DFT contract

DFT:    X[k] = ∑n=0N-1 x[n] e-j2πkn/N,   k = 0, ..., N - 1 IDFT:   x[n] = (1/N) ∑k=0N-1 X[k] ej2πkn/N Bin frequency:   fk = k fs / N

A longer window gives finer frequency resolution but poorer time localization. A shorter window tracks transients better but spreads frequency estimates.

Windowing

The DFT assumes the analyzed frame repeats periodically. If the frame edges do not match, spectral leakage appears. Window functions such as Hann, Hamming, and Blackman taper the edges to reduce leakage.

  • Rectangular: sharpest main lobe, highest leakage.
  • Hann: good general-purpose audio analysis window.
  • Blackman: lower sidelobes, wider main lobe.

DFT as a matrix

The DFT can be written as a matrix multiplication. Every output bin is an inner product between the signal and one complex sinusoid. Direct DFT therefore costs about N^2 complex multiply-adds. That is acceptable for tiny signals but expensive for long audio windows.

WN = e-j2π/N X[k] = ∑n=0N-1 x[n] WNkn X = FN x, where FN[k,n] = WNkn The DFT basis vectors are rotating arrows. Magnitude says how strongly the signal matches a basis frequency; phase says the alignment.

FFT algorithm

The fast Fourier transform is not a different transform. It is a faster way to compute the same DFT by exploiting symmetry and periodicity in the complex roots of unity. The most common radix-2 Cooley-Tukey FFT splits the signal into even-indexed and odd-indexed samples, recursively transforms each half, then combines them with twiddle factors.

X[k] = E[k] + WNk O[k] X[k + N/2] = E[k] - WNk O[k] E is the DFT of even samples. O is the DFT of odd samples. Reusing them reduces cost from O(N^2) to O(N log N). 
radix2_fft(x):
    if length(x) == 1:
        return x
    E = radix2_fft(even-indexed samples of x)
    O = radix2_fft(odd-indexed samples of x)
    for k in 0 .. N/2 - 1:
        t = exp(-j*2*pi*k/N) * O[k]
        X[k]       = E[k] + t
        X[k + N/2] = E[k] - t
    return X

Block processing and FFT convolution

Real-time DSP rarely processes an infinite signal at once. Audio arrives in blocks, such as 64, 128, or 512 samples. Short FIR filters are often cheaper in direct time-domain form. Long convolution, such as cabinet simulation or room reverb, is usually cheaper in the frequency domain by overlap-add or overlap-save.

Time-domain direct convolution cost grows like   N M FFT convolution cost grows roughly like   N log2 N For long impulse responses, transforming blocks to frequency space, multiplying spectra, and transforming back can be much faster.

7. Interesting DSP Techniques

Many practical audio effects are combinations of delays, filters, nonlinearities, modulation, transforms, and time-varying parameters.

Technique Core operation Audio use Watch out for
Delay and echo Add delayed copies of a signal. Echo, slapback, comb filtering. Feedback can grow if gain is too high.
Reverb Dense delay network or convolution with room impulse response. Room and space simulation. Long impulse responses can be expensive without FFT convolution.
Dynamic range compression Time-varying gain from envelope follower. Loudness control, vocals, drums, mastering. Attack and release artifacts.
Modulation Multiply by or vary with a low-frequency oscillator. Tremolo, vibrato, ring modulation, AM synthesis. Sidebands and aliasing if modulation is too fast.
Quantization and dither Round samples to discrete levels; optionally add small noise. Bit depth reduction, digital storage. Quantization distortion without dither.
Resampling Lowpass, interpolate, and decimate. Changing sample rate. Aliasing if anti-aliasing filter is skipped.

Linear, nonlinear, and time-varying systems

Convolution exactly describes linear time-invariant systems. Many musical effects are not fully LTI. Distortion is nonlinear, so it creates new frequencies. Tremolo, chorus, flanging, and phasing are time-varying, so their behavior changes over time. Compressors are nonlinear and time-varying because their gain depends on the recent signal envelope. LTI analysis is still useful, but it is not the whole story.

LTI filter:   y = x * h Nonlinear shaper:   y(t) = tanh(g x(t)) Time-varying gain:   y(t) = a(t) x(t) Nonlinear and time-varying systems can create sidebands, harmonics, clicks, and aliasing that do not appear in ordinary LTI convolution.

9. Debugging DSP Systems

DSP bugs usually show up as clipping, aliasing, instability, phase cancellation, latency, or state discontinuities. Draw and listen before guessing.

Symptom Likely cause Check
Harsh new tones after downsampling Aliasing. Lowpass before decimation and inspect spectrum.
Output explodes or becomes NaN IIR instability or feedback gain too high. Check pole radii, coefficient signs, and internal state.
Filtered sound is delayed FIR group delay or lookahead processing. Measure impulse response peak and compensate latency.
Comb filtering when mixing channels Delayed copy or phase mismatch. Inspect phase and delay alignment.
Clicks at block boundaries Discontinuous state, parameter jumps, or buffer splice. Preserve filter state and smooth parameters.
