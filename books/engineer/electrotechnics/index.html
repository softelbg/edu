Electrotechnics: Circuits, Fields, and Maxwell Laws

An academic, visual introduction to electrical engineering foundations: Kirchhoff laws, circuit energy, complex impedance, phasors, Lorentz force, divergence and curl, integral and differential Maxwell equations, induction, and electromagnetic fields.

Circuits as constraints.
Use KCL and KVL to solve currents, voltages, power, and equivalent impedance.
Fields as geometry.
Read divergence, curl, flux, circulation, and Lorentz force as measurable structure.
Maxwell as unification.
Connect charge, current, electric fields, magnetic fields, induction, and displacement current.
electric field magnetic field current

Electrotechnics starts by separating circuit variables from field variables. Circuit theory uses lumped voltages and currents. Field theory describes space with electric and magnetic vector fields. The two descriptions agree when the geometry is small compared with the electromagnetic wavelength and when parasitic field storage is controlled.

Lumped versus distributed view

A circuit element is an approximation of an electromagnetic field configuration. A resistor converts field energy into heat, a capacitor stores electric field energy, an inductor stores magnetic field energy, and wires constrain potential differences while carrying charge. At high frequencies or large dimensions, wires become transmission lines and the field view becomes necessary.

2. Circuit Laws

Kirchhoff laws are conservation statements for lumped circuits. KCL conserves charge at a node. KVL follows from a conservative electric field in a lumped circuit without time-varying magnetic flux threading the loop.

Kirchhoff Current Law

Algebraic node form ∑k ik = 0 Equivalent statement ∑ ientering = ∑ ileaving

KCL is a low-frequency statement of charge conservation. If charge accumulated at an ideal node, node voltage would change and the circuit assumption would be incomplete. In full field theory, continuity is ∇ · J = −∂ρ / ∂t.

Kirchhoff Voltage Law

Closed-loop voltage form ∑k vk = 0 Rise/drop statement ∑ vrises = ∑ vdrops

KVL is valid when the changing magnetic flux linked by the loop is negligible or explicitly represented by an element such as an inductor or transformer. Faraday's law is the field-level correction.

3. Field Operators: Gradient, Divergence, Curl

Vector calculus is the grammar of electromagnetic fields. The same field can be described locally by differential operators or globally by integrals over curves, surfaces, and volumes.

Operator Symbol Geometric meaning Electrotechnics use
Gradient grad φ = ∇φ Direction and rate of greatest increase of a scalar field. Electrostatic field from potential: E = −∇V.
Divergence div F = ∇ · F Net outward flux density per unit volume. Charge as source of D; no magnetic monopoles for B.
Curl curl F = ∇ × F Local circulation density and rotation axis. Induction and magnetic fields around current.
Laplacian ∇2φ Divergence of gradient. Laplace/Poisson equations for electrostatic potential.

Integral theorems

Divergence theorem ∫V (∇ · F) dV = ∯S F · n dS Local source density integrates to total outward flux.
Stokes theorem ∫S (∇ × F) · n dS = ∮C F · dl Local curl integrates to circulation around the boundary.

4. Maxwell Equations

Maxwell's equations connect sources, fields, and time variation. The integral forms are tied directly to measurable fluxes and circulations. The differential forms describe the local field at each point.

Law Integral form Differential form Physical meaning
Gauss law for electricity ∯S D · dS = Qfree,enclosed ∇ · D = ρfree Free charge is the source or sink of electric flux density.
Gauss law for magnetism ∯S B · dS = 0 ∇ · B = 0 Magnetic flux has no isolated source; field lines close.
Faraday induction law ∮C E · dl = − d/dt ∫S B · dS ∇ × E = −∂B / ∂t Changing magnetic flux induces circulating electric field and EMF.
Ampere-Maxwell law ∮C H · dl = ∫S Jfree · dS + d/dt ∫S D · dS ∇ × H = Jfree + ∂D / ∂t Current and changing electric flux produce magnetic circulation.

Continuity and displacement current

Taking divergence of Ampere-Maxwell gives charge conservation because ∇ · (∇ × H) = 0 and ∇ · D = ρ. The displacement-current term is not optional in time-varying fields; it makes current continuity work through capacitors and enables electromagnetic waves.

∇ · Jfree + ∂ρfree / ∂t = 0

Wave implication in a uniform lossless medium

In a source-free, homogeneous medium with D = εE and B = μH, Maxwell equations imply wave equations. The propagation speed is v = 1 / √(με), which becomes the speed of light in vacuum.

∇2E = με ∂2E / ∂t2 ∇2H = με ∂2H / ∂t2

5. Materials, Boundaries, and Energy

Real electrotechnical systems are shaped by materials. Conductors, dielectrics, magnetic cores, insulation, air gaps, losses, and saturation determine the practical behavior of circuits and machines.

Conduction currentJ = σEOhmic conduction in material.Temperature dependence, skin effect, semiconductors.
Poynting vectorS = E × HElectromagnetic power flow density.Needs field geometry, not only lumped variables.

Boundary conditions

n · (D2 − D1) = ρs,free n · (B2 − B1) = 0 n × (E2 − E1) = 0 n × (H2 − H1) = Js,free

These boundary conditions explain why conductors become equipotential in electrostatics, why flux continuity matters in magnetic cores, why insulation thickness affects breakdown, and why high-frequency current crowds near conductor surfaces.

6. Sinusoidal Steady State and Complex Impedance

When sources are sinusoidal at one angular frequency ω, linear circuit differential equations become algebraic equations in complex phasors. This is the main bridge from calculus to practical AC circuit solving.

Phasors

A sinusoid can be represented by a complex number storing magnitude and phase. The physical signal is the real part.

v(t) = ℜ{ V ejωt } i(t) = ℜ{ I ejωt }

Impedance

Impedance generalizes resistance. Its real part consumes average power; its imaginary part stores and returns energy each cycle.

ZR = R ZL = jωL ZC = 1 / (jωC) V = ZI

Series and parallel combinations

Series Zeq = Z1 + Z2 + … + Zn Parallel 1 / Zeq = 1 / Z1 + 1 / Z2 + … + 1 / Zn

Complex impedance problems are not only arithmetic. The engineering interpretation matters: resonance occurs when inductive and capacitive reactances cancel, current may become large, power factor changes apparent power demand, and frequency response determines filtering behavior.

7. Energy, Lorentz Force, and Electromechanical Conversion

Forces and machines connect fields to motion. A charge in electric and magnetic fields experiences Lorentz force. A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force. A changing flux through a coil induces voltage. These are the foundations of motors, generators, transformers, relays, loudspeakers, and sensors.

Lorentz force

F = q(E + v × B)

Electric field accelerates charge parallel to E. Magnetic force is perpendicular to both velocity and magnetic flux density, so it changes direction of motion without doing work on a point charge by itself.

Force on conductor

dF = I dl × B Straight conductor F = ILB sin θ

The direction follows the vector cross product. Reversing current or magnetic field reverses force.

Induction and flux linkage

Φ = ∫S B · dS λ = NΦ e = −dλ / dt

The negative sign is Lenz's law: induced current opposes the change in magnetic flux that created it. This is a conservation-of-energy statement, not just a sign convention.

9. Problem-Solving Method

Electrotechnics problems become tractable when you choose the correct abstraction: lumped circuit, phasor circuit, static field, quasistatic induction, wave propagation, or electromechanical energy conversion.

General checklist

  1. Draw the reference directions for currents, voltages, fields, normals, and loop orientation.
  2. State the approximation: lumped, sinusoidal steady-state, static field, quasistatic field, or wave.
  3. Write conservation laws before substituting numbers.
  4. Check units at every derived expression.
  5. Evaluate limiting cases: zero frequency, infinite frequency, open circuit, short circuit, no source, no loss.
  6. Verify energy: sources must equal storage change plus dissipation plus mechanical work.
